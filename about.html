About

Beyond the Portfolio

The experiences that shaped my approach to learning, leadership, and performance.

From performance to performance improvement.

My approach to learning design was shaped long before I began building formal L&D projects. As a performer, educator, conductor, and facilitator, I learned how people develop confidence, respond to feedback, build skills over time, and work toward meaningful outcomes.

In music, growth is visible. You can hear when a learner begins with uncertainty, gains confidence through guided practice, and eventually performs with independence and expression. That experience shaped the way I think about learning: it should be structured, human, supportive, and connected to a real performance goal.

Learning Philosophy

Confidence unlocks every other part of learning.

The most effective learning experiences create clarity, reduce barriers, and help learners believe they can succeed before they ever demonstrate mastery.

My approach

Designing for confidence, not just completion.

I am drawn to learning experiences that help people move from “I understand this” to “I can actually do this.” That means designing clear expectations, practice opportunities, feedback loops, reflection points, and performance supports that help learners apply new skills beyond the training environment.

The throughline

From rehearsal rooms to learning systems.

Whether I was leading a choir, building onboarding materials, designing a digital media pathway, or developing an interactive portfolio experience, the goal was similar: understand the learner, remove barriers, support practice, and create conditions where people can perform with greater confidence.

Design philosophy

What guides the work.

Clarity first

Learners need to know what success looks like before they can work toward it.

Practice with purpose

Skill development happens through structured practice, feedback, and application.

Reflect and improve

Effective learning systems evolve based on learner needs, performance data, and feedback.

People before tools

Technology matters most when it removes barriers and helps learners do something meaningful.

Performance matters

Learning should prepare people to apply skills in real contexts, not just complete activities.

Support the transfer

Job aids, coaching, reflection, and feedback help learning move beyond the lesson itself.
